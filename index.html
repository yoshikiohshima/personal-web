Computer Scientist/Software Engineer

About Me

I have been working on creating new programming languages and environments, designing and implementing dynamic interactive systems and applications, especially for the education field. I have published dozens of academic papers and served a program committee member for conferences and workshops.

I was working on an optimizing compiler project and also a research prototype of a programming language with nested transactions at the graduate school of Tokyo Institute of Technology. It was fine... but looking back, I think was feeling it was a bit too narrowly focused but I did not know how much a bigger picture was there in the field of computing. It all changed when an interesting twist changed the course of my life.

As shown below, I got to closely work with Dr. Alan Kay and his group over decades. There are questions like "what is computing?" in the Koan-like sense that "what is computing if you take away all hardware in front of your eyes?", and "what is means to have computing for humanity?". The group was not only talk about those stuff, but also were actually making those ideas concrete.

I consider myself an "above-average" programmer but I have been trying to learn about things. I think that the knowledge and outlook I accumulated over time are making some differences on what I create.

Software Projects

Rie and Jun compiler construction project (1993-)

Under supervision of Professor Masataka Sassa, I worked on a compiler construction project. The project's aim was to write all stages of an optimizing compiler in declarative forms, especially in variations of Attribute Grammar.
  • Rie and Jun

The Harmony/2 Language (1995-)

Under supervision of Dr. Ken Wakita, I worked on an object-oriented language with nested transaction.
  • Concurrent Transactions and Communicators: Extensible Synchronization Mechanisms for Distributed Programming

Squeak Smalltalk (1997-)

I attended OOPSLA 1997 in Atlanta as a student volunteer. An interesting twist of fate got me in touch with John Maloney of the Squeak Team (via Professor Satoshi Matusoka) at the reception hall, and Prof. Matsuoka volunteered me to try porting Squeak to a PDA called Zaurus from the Sharp corporation in Japan that Prof. Matsuoka was carrying (and showing off). I only had very vague idea of what Smalltalk was about. I was familiar with Emacs, and had some sense that an interactive and scriptable computing system as a whole is good... But I was totally blown away that the entire system (really) can be all coherently created as a dynamic system . Since then, many things I do in computing have been influenced by the notion of whole system design, dynamic modification of a living system and education aspect of computing all comes with Smalltalk and Squeak.

Porting Squeak to Zaurus PDA (1998)

Squeak was designed to be extremely portable so that it can run on wide variety of hardware platforms, including low-power handheld devices. I started working on porting Squeak to Zaurus in 1998, and thanks to lucky coincidences (Sharp just released a public SDK, and the latest hardware just barely got ennough memory, etc), I managed to make the port work. For this, and some other contributions I made for the Squeak Project, I was invited by Dr. Alan Kay's group to visit Walt Disney Imagineering R&D in Los Angeles and to OOPSLA that was held in Vancouver that year. It also marked my first occasion to make a transit at an airport (SFO), and I missed the plane.

Porting NetFront Web browser to Zaurus PDA (2000)

The ACCESS company was spearheading the mobile web industry with their NetFront browser. Not unlike Squeak, NetFront was architected so that it can be ported to a new platform by supplying platform-dependent code. I blatantly re-purposed some of the code I wrote for Squeak to the NetFront browser. But, hey, it is some open source code I wrote anyway.

Parks PDA(2000)

Walt Disney Imagineering R&D was interested in emerging portable electronic devices (Personal Digital Assistants) and exploring what they can do with them to enhace guest experiences. Around that time, a model of Zaurus PDA had a unique device that had 1) an out-door viewable color screen, 2) enough performance to play audio and frame-based animation at the quality that Disney requires, 3) an external storage slot to store media-rich content, and most notably 4) a dynamic object system called Squeak that allowed us to make a new custom user interface framework that in tur allowed the designers to create high quality content quickly. We spent two weeks in late October and November in Orlando and stayed at an affiliated hotel. Once the test started going smoothly, I got a lot of free time and went all those parks and cut the queue lines with my R&D badge. We anticipated some news coverage of our test, but the Bush-Gore thing happened and there was no space on newspaper for that kind of fun stuff.
  • The Parks PDA: A Handheld Device for Theme Park Guests in Squeak

Stitch's Photo Phone (2002)

This is an experimental of a new interactive theme park attraction. In a phone booth large enough to fit a family, we put a large flat screen as one of the walls. A cast member can puppet a 3D character (Stitch) real time with a game controller and chat with the family, and at the end of a short session, Stitch takes a photo of the family, and give a printed version the family. I worked on the code that captures the image from a hidden video camera. The experiment won the 10th annual Themed Entertainment Association Award. The test with the actual guest was done at the Disneyland in Anaheim, and for various reasons, I basically went there from Glendale three weeks in a row.

Etoys (2003)

At Disney, I was officially working on other projects (like the ones mentioned above and others) than Squeak itself (though I was using it for some projects and contributed to it). The 9/11 attack also hit the bottom line of the Disney company, and the Squeak team eventually were laid off. I was kept a bit longer than the Squeak team members but eventually also let go. I would have to return to Japan due to the visa restriction, but thanks to Dr. Masanobu Matsuo, I got a positioon at his company called Twin Sun. I was more and more closely in the projects of Viewpoints Research Institute, which Alan Kay and Kim Rose founded. A major project of Viewpoints was Etoys, which is the end-user authoring environment (built on top of Squeak). From around this time, I was involved in improving and extending Etoys and over time took the leading role of the project.

Kedama (2005)

Following Mitch Resnick's StarLogo and John Maloney's StarSqueak. I created a massively parallel particle simulation system that is integrated with Etoys. In this way, thousands of particles can be controlled by Etoys block scripting and their behavior can be changed on the fly. This was the basis of my Ph.D. thesis.
  • Kedama: A GUI-based Interactive Massively Parallel Particle Programming System

Squeak Version of Croquet (2006)

While working on Squeak, my colleagues David Smith, Andreas Raab, David Reed and Alan Kay started a realtime 3D collaboration environment called Croquet. I was on and off contributing to it, (I even wrote a prototype of Paxos during the exploration phase). Some ideas were made into papers.
  • Filters and Tasks in Croquet

Tweak (2006)

Andreas Raab led the effort to create a language system called Tweak One of the goals of Tweak was to make a new generation of Etoys. I created a version of particle system, and helped debugging the end-user program editing system in Tweak.

Etoys for OLPC (2008-)

The OLPC (One Laptop per Child) project aimed to create an inexpensive laptop that is designed to help children around the world. Our group was involved in early discussions of software design. There was an idea to use Squeak as the main foundation for the laptop so that the user (chidren) can engage a "learning by doing" style activity. That plan did not pan out, but we put considerable effort to make the Etoys run as an application. Because there were not much applications for OLPC where users can actually make things, Etoys was popular for certain teachers and learners. I was taking the leading role of the effort.
  • Smalltalk development on OLPC
  • Etoys for One Laptop Per Child

The STEPS Project (2007-2013)

Viewpoints Research received a 5-year grant from the National Science Foundatation to demonstrate the feasibility of an idea that the kernel of an entire personal computing system can be written in about 20,000 lines of code. That includes the compiler, the network driver, the memory management system, the graphics system, text formatting algorithm, application framework and applications. It was a large group effort, and various members came up with many interesting ideas and systems. I took on the task to make an application framework, and I chose Functional Reactive Programming as its basis, but with a twist of allowng dynamic modification of running code. Also, we designed the framework so that all "applications" on the framework can be embedded in to another. The declarative nature of reactive descriptionn, and not having distinction between application and widgets allowed as to make a universal authorinng application.
  • The STEP Project proposal
  • The GUI framework for the STEP project

GP (2013-)

With John Maloney (who was indeed the lead developr of Scratch) and Jens Moenig (of Snap!), I worked on a new blocks-based programming system called GP. Three of us combined had considerable experiences in making various end-user programming systems, but we had been bothered by the fact that none of those systems we created were truly "general purpose", in a sense that it is powerful enough to write complex applications, such as the blocks-based programming systems itself. John was the main architect, and I worked on various parts such as designing a module system, porting the virtual machine to 64-bit platforms, etc.
  • GP: General Purpose Blocks Language
  • A Module System for a General-Purpose Blocks Language

Kanto (2017)

I was interested in computer-based collaboration, especially a place where people can make something together and learn from each other. It occurred to me that a screensharing with WebRTC and a channel where a remote user can send events, we can create a collaborative environment for different blacks-based editors. I modified the Snap! editor to allow multiple pointers and hook up the WebRTC based collaboration system to have a functional collaborative blocks-eiting environment. The same collaboration framework can be used to make SqueakJS and web version of GP collaborative as well. The name of the system is Kanto. The tradition of this kind of collaboration space was started by a system called Kansas, named after a US state that is a large rectagular flat area. Squeak followed its own implemtation called Nebraska. As I was working with Jens, I thought it might be fun to pick a flat area in Germany.. but when he told me that it would be "Schleswig-Holstein", a German name probably was not going to fly (or even unpronounciable for many people) I picked "Kanto" in Japan as the name of the system.
  • Kanto: A Multi-participant Screen-Sharing System for Etoys, Snap!, and GP

    JavaScript version of Croquet (2017-)

    When Y Combinator Research could no longer support our research activity, David Smith and some of my colleagues, such as Dan Ingalls, Vanessa Freudenberg and Aran Lunzer, formed a start up company. The main goal was to have the realtime collaboration technology going on the Web. The fundamental algorithm was devised decades earlier by the original Croquet group. We reimplemented the algorithm in JavaScript and for JavaScript, and created an SDK so that a realtime collaborative application can be created easily. While the SDK provides a good foundation, making a larger application that requires dynamic DOM manipulation and scriping them needs a higher-level abstraction. I designed and created a virtual DOM based application framework (with help from colleagues, certainly!). I also architected and wrote majority of code of the shared space application called Q.
    • Croquet Virtual DOM Framework
